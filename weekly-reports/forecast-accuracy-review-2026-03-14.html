Key Weather Insight

The model's largest errors occurred on weather-transition days. Monday March 9 was coded as "Thunderstorm" in Houston but received only 0.016" of actual precipitation — customers treated it as a near-dry day. The model over-suppressed volume for the threatening forecast, resulting in massive under-prediction. Wednesday's rain day showed the opposite: the model over-predicted because it didn't suppress enough for the 0.22" actual rain. The model needs better calibration between forecast weather codes and actual precipitation impact on wash behavior.

Emerging Pattern: Post-Rain Bounce-Back Blindspot

The model's worst errors both occurred on days immediately following rain events (Monday after Sunday's storm, Thursday after Wednesday's heavy rain). In both cases, the model under-predicted by 21–49%. This suggests the model lacks a "pent-up demand" or "bounce-back" feature — customers who skip a wash day due to rain often return in higher-than-normal numbers the following day. Adding a lagged rain indicator or a "days since last rain" feature could materially improve post-rain accuracy.

Retraining Decision

No Retraining — Monitoring Mode

While this week's MAPE figures are elevated above baseline for the TX sites (TX160: 25.1% vs 18% baseline, TX190: 32.1% vs 18% baseline), the statistical decision rules do not support retraining at this time:

EWMA Threshold (baseline + 5pp, 2+ weeks) NOT TRIGGERED — First week of tracking
CUSUM Bias (±3 sigma) CANNOT EVALUATE — Insufficient history
Consecutive Same-Direction Bias (4+ weeks) NOT TRIGGERED — Week 1 of 4 needed
Catastrophic Week (TX > 40%, UT > 55%) NOT TRIGGERED — TX160: 25.1%, TX190: 32.1%, UT426: 30.1%

Rationale: Regression to the mean is likely. Monday's 49% fleet error was driven by a weather-code misinterpretation (thunderstorm forecast with negligible actual precipitation), not a model structural failure. Excluding Monday, the remaining 3 days posted a reasonable 15.3% fleet MAPE. One bad data point should not trigger costly retraining. If next week also shows elevated under-prediction bias, the EWMA threshold will trigger and retraining will proceed.

Recommendations
1. Add Post-Rain Bounce Feature
The model's biggest blind spot is post-rain demand recovery. Consider adding a "precipitation_yesterday" or "days_since_rain" feature to capture pent-up demand patterns. This could reduce Monday-type misses by 20–30pp.
2. Investigate TX190 Thursday Surge
TX190 posted 315 washes on Thursday — a 49% WoW surge and far above the 173 predicted. Check for promotional activity, pollen events, or local factors driving Webster traffic that the model doesn't capture.
3. Calibrate Weather Code Sensitivity
The model treats "thunderstorm" weather codes as heavy suppressors, but Monday's 0.016" actual precipitation shows the code overstates the impact. Consider using precipitation amount (not code) as the primary weather feature.
4. UT426 Model Performing Near Baseline
UT426 at 30.1% MAPE is within its 32% baseline — no action needed. Bias is near-neutral (-2.4%), suggesting the model captures Utah patterns adequately despite higher overall variance.
5. Continue Weekly Tracking
This is Week 1 of the SPC framework. At least 4 weeks of data are needed before EWMA/CUSUM signals become statistically meaningful. Maintain weekly reviews to build the evidence base for informed retraining decisions.
6. UT426 Flat-Line Bug (Mar 9 Briefing)
The March 9 briefing predicted exactly 130 washes for UT426 across all 5 forecast days — a clear flat-line output suggesting a model fallback or error for that site on that run. Investigate the forecast.py code path for this edge case.
