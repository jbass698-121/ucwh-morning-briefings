FTC Delivers AI Policy Statement on Schedule — Federal Preemption Battle with States Now Live

Wilson Sonsini / King & Spalding / Federal News Network · Mar 11 · HIGH

The FTC delivered its March 11 policy statement on schedule, describing how the FTC Act applies to AI and when state laws requiring alteration of truthful AI outputs are preempted by federal law. Simultaneously, the Secretary of Commerce published an evaluation identifying "burdensome" state AI laws that conflict with federal policy and merit referral to the AI Litigation Task Force. This sets up the defining legal battle of 2026: whether AI governance centralizes under a federal framework (as the Trump administration's December executive order envisioned) or remains a patchwork of state laws led by California's TFAIA and Texas's RAIGA, both now in active enforcement. For companies deploying AI agents, the practical question is whether to comply with the strictest state laws or bet on federal preemption — a gamble with real legal exposure either way.

Why this matters for you: The dual-track federal/state AI governance landscape creates compliance uncertainty that directly affects how you deploy agents in production. If you're building agentic systems that operate across state lines, the safest path is designing for the strictest applicable standard (California's TFAIA) while tracking the preemption litigation. The FTC statement also clarifies that AI systems generating misleading outputs face existing consumer protection liability — a risk for any agent system that communicates with customers or generates customer-facing content.

South Korea's AI Basic Act Takes Effect — First Comprehensive AI Framework in Asia

Pearl Cohen / Governance Intelligence / Baker Donelson · Jan 22 · MEDIUM

South Korea's AI Basic Act became enforceable on January 22, 2026, making it the first country in Asia with a comprehensive AI legislative framework. The law establishes risk-based classifications for AI systems, mandatory transparency obligations, and governance requirements for high-risk applications. Coming alongside California's TFAIA, Texas's RAIGA, and the EU's delayed high-risk provisions, the global regulatory landscape is fragmenting into distinct regional approaches: the EU's precautionary model, America's emerging federal-vs-state tension, and now Asia's first comprehensive framework. For companies operating globally, the compliance surface area is expanding rapidly — each jurisdiction requires different documentation, risk assessments, and disclosure obligations for AI systems.
